MDD(Major Depressive Disorder)

       Major Depressive Disorder is a common disorder. It is the result of a chemical imbalance in the brain. The imbalance makes it difficult for the person to regulate their emotions. Common symptoms of MDD are persistent sadness, heavy loss of interest, feelings of worthlessness and/or guilt, and hopelessness. MDD is a constant sadness, not to be confused with being sad, which is temporary.

What Causes MDD

       There are many different ways MDD can form, but around 8.3% of adults have had MDD. MDD is partially a genetic illness, but can occur if depression goes untreated, and gets much worse. It is a chemical imbalance in the brain, causing uncommon reactions to stimuli, much like depression. Although it is a chemical imbalance, environmental factors can cause it to worsen.

Demographics Affected

       16% of females have MDD while 10% of males have MDD. Approximately 21.0 million adults in the U.S. had at least one major depressive episode in 2021, representing 8.3% of all U.S. Females are more prone to the disorder than males. It is most common in young adults aged 18-25 according to NIMH. It is also most common in people who have 2 or more races, being 13.9% of individuals. 61% of US adults who are suffering with MDD reached out and got help. 20% of the US population aged 12-17 have had atleast one MDD episode. That is around 5.0 million adolescents. It was more common in girls than boys.
